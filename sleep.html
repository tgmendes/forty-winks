Your Baby's Sleep

A science-based quick reference guide for tired parents. Tap your baby's age below to jump right to what you need.

Based on: The Happiest Baby on the Block · Precious Little Sleep · Healthy Sleep Habits, Happy Child
⭐ Golden Rule

Sleep begets sleep. An overtired baby is harder to soothe and sleep-train than a well-rested one. Never sacrifice a nap to improve night sleep — it backfires.

Age-by-Age Sleep Guide
Weeks 1–4

The Fourth Trimester Begins

Survival mode — no schedule, just response
  • Sleeps 14–18 hours per day in short bursts (45 min–3 hrs)
  • No circadian rhythm yet — day and night are the same to your baby
  • Wake windows: only 45–60 minutes between sleeps
  • Feeding every 2–3 hours (breast) or 2.5–3.5 hrs (formula) — this drives sleep cycles
  • Startle (Moro) reflex wakes baby frequently — this is normal and neurological
  • Stomach is the size of a marble at birth, a walnut at 1 week — tiny feeds, frequent waking
  • Feed on demand — do not attempt any schedule yet
  • Allow all sleep props: feeding to sleep, holding, rocking, pacifier — ALL acceptable now
  • Swaddle every sleep to suppress Moro reflex
  • Keep room dark and use white noise for all sleeps
  • Safe sleep: firm flat surface, on back, no loose bedding (AAP guidelines)
  • Do not worry about ‘bad habits’ — survival and bonding come first
⭐ The 5 S's (Dr. Karp) — Start Now

Swaddle → Side/Stomach hold → Shush → Swing → Suck. Layer all 5 simultaneously for a crying newborn. Each one activates the calming reflex; together they are dramatically more effective.

Weeks 4–8

First Patterns Emerge

Tiny circadian windows opening
  • Sleep total: 14–16 hours/day, still no consolidated night sleep
  • Wake windows extend slightly to 60–75 minutes
  • First ‘social smile’ appears (~6 weeks) — baby is more alert and stimulated
  • 6-week peak fussiness and sleep regression is common — this is neurological, not your fault
  • Around 6–8 weeks: you may see a first longer night stretch of 3–5 hours
  • Naps are still short (30–45 min) and unpredictable
  • Continue all 5 S's for soothing
  • Begin differentiating day and night: bright light and activity during day, dim/dark and quiet at night
  • Introduce a simple ‘pre-sleep ritual’ (even 2 steps): e.g., dim lights → swaddle → white noise
  • Watch wake windows carefully — overtiredness is the #1 enemy this stage
  • Consider a ‘EASY’ loose structure: Eat, Activity, Sleep, You-time (but do NOT enforce rigidly)
  • Keep doing what works — do not sleep train yet
Months 2–3

Circadian Rhythm Developing

Longer nights begin to appear
  • Sleep total: 13–15 hours/day
  • Wake windows: 75–90 minutes
  • Circadian rhythm begins forming — melatonin production increases
  • Naps consolidate slightly: 3–5 naps/day, some may lengthen to 60–90 min
  • Many babies begin giving a 4–6 hour first stretch at night
  • ~8–12 week growth spurt: temporary increase in night waking and feeding
  • Swaddle still essential — Moro reflex usually gone by 3–4 months
  • Set a consistent bedtime between 7–9 PM (follow drowsy cues, not the clock)
  • Establish a 20–30 min bedtime routine: bath → massage → feed → dark room → white noise → sleep
  • Practice putting baby down ‘drowsy but awake’ once or twice per day — builds self-soothing foundation
  • Introduce light/dark cues: open curtains after morning wake, blackout blinds for all sleeps
  • Begin weaning the swaddle only if baby is showing signs of rolling (safety critical)
⭐ ‘Drowsy But Awake’ — Plant the Seed Now

You do not need baby to fall asleep independently yet. But practicing putting them down drowsy (not fully asleep) 1–2x per day at this age makes sleep training much easier at 4–6 months. Think of it as low-stakes practice.

Months 3–4

The 4-Month Regression

The most disruptive — and most misunderstood — regression
  • Sleep architecture permanently shifts to adult-like cycles (light → deep → light every ~45 min)
  • Baby now fully wakes between sleep cycles and needs help connecting them
  • Previously good sleepers suddenly wake every 1–2 hours — this is NOT a phase that passes on its own
  • Naps may fall apart — short 30–45 min naps are common
  • Wake windows extend to 90–120 minutes
  • This regression does NOT resolve without some change in approach
  • Understand this is permanent brain development — do not wait it out hoping it resolves
  • Begin actively working toward independent sleep at bedtime
  • Transition out of swaddle now if not already done — use arms-out transition swaddle for 1 week
  • Introduce a consistent 4-nap schedule: wake → nap 1 (1.5 hr wake window) → nap 2 → nap 3 → nap 4 → bedtime
  • Move bedtime earlier if needed — 7–7:30 PM is often magic for this age
  • Consider pacifier use (if not already): it is AAP-approved and reduces SIDS risk
⭐ Why This Regression Is Different

The 4-month regression is permanent neurological change, not a temporary blip. Babies who were rocked/fed to sleep now wake and expect that same input every 45 minutes all night. The only lasting solution is teaching independent sleep skills.

Months 4–6

Prime Sleep Training Window

The optimal time to build independent sleep
  • Sleep total: 12–15 hours/day (10–12 hrs night + 2–3 hrs naps)
  • Wake windows: 1.5–2.5 hours (gradually increasing)
  • 3–4 naps at 4 months, transitioning to 3 naps by 5–6 months
  • Biologically ready for longer night stretches — many can go 8–12 hours without feeding
  • Developmental leaps and growth spurts may temporarily disrupt sleep
  • Solid foods begin ~6 months — no evidence this improves sleep
  • This is the ideal window for sleep training
  • Establish firm consistent schedule: morning wake time anchors the whole day
  • Nap schedule: Nap 1 ~1.5–2 hrs after morning wake; Nap 2 ~1.5–2 hrs after Nap 1; Nap 3 (catnap) late afternoon
  • Room transition: moving to own room works well at this age — easier before object permanence solidifies
  • Drop night feeds gradually (consult pediatrician on nutritional needs first)
  • Do not reintroduce sleep props you have weaned — consistency is critical
Months 6–9

Separation Anxiety Begins

Object permanence and attachment intensify sleep challenges
  • Sleep total: 12–14 hours/day
  • Wake windows: 2.5–3.5 hours
  • 2–3 naps, transitioning to 2 naps around 6–8 months
  • Object permanence develops (~7–8 months): baby now knows you still exist when you leave
  • Separation anxiety peaks — bedtime protests may increase even for previously good sleepers
  • 6-month sleep regression linked to developmental leaps (sitting, crawling)
  • Play peekaboo games during the day — this teaches object permanence and that ‘gone’ means ‘coming back’
  • Keep bedtime routine ultra-consistent — predictability is security
  • If sleep training hasn't happened, it becomes harder but is still very achievable now
  • Drop to 2 naps when wake windows exceed 2.5 hours or nap 1 keeps pushing back
  • Watch for overtiredness from 2-nap transition — move bedtime earlier during transition
Months 9–12

Mastery & The Final Nap Transition

Consolidation toward 2-nap then 1-nap schedule
  • Sleep total: 12–14 hours/day (10–12 hrs night + 2–3 hrs naps)
  • Wake windows: 3–4 hours
  • Steadily on 2 naps; some babies begin 2-to-1 nap transition near 12–15 months
  • 9-month regression: linked to major motor development (pulling to stand, cruising)
  • Night wakings may return with teething — distinguish teething from habit-based waking
  • Early rising (before 6 AM) becomes common — do not rush in; use white noise and dark room
  • Maintain consistent 2-nap schedule with firm morning wake anchor
  • Address early rising by ensuring total daytime sleep is not too high and bedtime is not too early
  • For teething: comfort briefly then return to crib — avoid re-establishing full sleep props
  • Continue white noise through this phase — it remains valuable for consolidating sleep
  • Begin using comfort object (lovey) — can help baby self-soothe when placed in crib consistently

Swaddling — In Depth

  • Use a large (47″) square muslin or stretchy swaddle blanket
  • Arms must be down at sides — arms-up swaddle is less effective for Moro suppression
  • Hips must be free to flex — hip dysplasia risk if legs swaddled straight
  • Stop swaddling immediately when baby shows any rolling signs — safety imperative
  • Transition: arms-out swaddle for 1 week → sleep sack
⭐ Swaddle Safety

A loose swaddle is dangerous. If baby can work arms free, the blanket becomes a suffocation hazard. Use velcro/zippered swaddle products (Love to Dream, Ollie, Swaddle UP) for reliability and safety.

White Noise

  • Use throughout entire first year and beyond — no evidence of harm from proper use
  • Volume: 65–70 dB at crib (like a shower running) — use a dB meter app to verify
  • Place machine 6+ feet from baby — never directly in crib
  • Use for ALL sleeps: naps and nights. Consistency matters more than type of sound
  • Pink noise and brown noise are also effective — slightly lower pitched

Pacifier

  • AAP-endorsed: reduces SIDS risk by up to 90% when used at sleep onset
  • Introduce after breastfeeding is established (3–4 weeks) if nursing
  • Do NOT replace in baby's mouth during night before 4–6 months — creates prop dependency
  • After 6 months: if pacifier waking occurs 4+ times/night, consider removing it entirely
  • Removal before 1 year has minimal dental impact

Key Decision Timeline

When to Move Baby to Their Own Room

  • AAP recommends room-sharing (not bed-sharing) for at least 6 months, ideally 1 year
  • Many parents find room-sharing disrupts their own sleep (and therefore baby's)
  • Research on the 6-month recommendation is debated — some studies show no SIDS increase after 3 months
  • Ideal transition window: 4–6 months — before object permanence, after initial SIDS risk period
  • Decision: discuss with your pediatrician and weigh family-specific factors

When to Sleep Train

  • Minimum age: 4 months (16+ weeks corrected age for premature babies)
  • Optimal window: 4–6 months — circadian rhythm established, before separation anxiety peaks
  • Still very effective: 6–9 months — takes 1–3 extra days
  • Harder but achievable: 9–12 months — object permanence + separation anxiety increases resistance
  • Prerequisites: pediatrician clears baby for night without feeds; baby is healthy
⭐ What the Research Says

A landmark 2012 study (Price et al., Pediatrics) and a 2016 Flinders University RCT (Hall et al.) found no lasting harm to infant attachment, stress levels, or parent-child relationship from controlled crying methods. Multiple Cochrane reviews support behavioral sleep interventions as safe and effective.

When to Drop Night Feeds

  • Always consult your pediatrician first — this is a medical decision
  • Most formula-fed babies: can manage without a feed from ~4 months if weight gain is adequate
  • Breastfed babies: may need 1–2 feeds until 6–9 months
  • Signs baby may be ready: gaining weight well, making up calories during day, feeds briefly at night
  • Method: cold turkey or gradual reduction (shorten feed by 1–2 min every 2 nights)

⭐ Transition Tip

During any nap transition: temporarily move bedtime 30–45 minutes earlier to compensate for lost daytime sleep. Expect 1–2 weeks of disrupted sleep during the transition.

Troubleshooting
  • Most likely cause: sleep cycle arousal — baby cannot connect cycles independently
  • Under 4 months: normal. Soothe and resettle. Not the time to ‘fix’ it
  • Over 4 months: this is a sleep associations issue. Baby needs same conditions at 3 AM that were present at bedtime
  • Fix: ensure bedtime conditions match middle-of-night conditions
  • Long-term fix: independent sleep at bedtime automatically resolves most night waking
  • Most common causes: bedtime too late (overtiredness paradox), room too bright, noise, too much daytime sleep
  • First check: blackout blinds — light before 6 AM is a primary culprit
  • Try moving bedtime 15–30 minutes earlier — counterintuitive but often works
  • White noise masks morning household sounds
  • Do not rush in before 6 AM — give 10–15 minutes even if baby fusses
  • Do not feed before 6 AM if your goal is to shift wake time
  • Under 4 months: normal architecture — one sleep cycle. Do not stress
  • 4–8 months: check wake windows — too short or too long both cause short naps
  • Ensure nap environment matches night: dark, white noise, sleep sack
  • Try to stretch: when baby wakes at 30 min, give 10 minutes before intervening
  • Nap extension: go in just BEFORE baby fully wakes at 25 min and resettle
  • 4 months: permanent architecture change — requires action
  • 6 months: developmental leaps — usually resolves in 2–4 weeks
  • 8–10 months: object permanence + motor development — 2–4 weeks
  • 12 months: nap transition + new mobility — 2–4 weeks
  • Hold firm on boundaries, offer extra comfort during the day, do not reintroduce weaned sleep props
  • Signs: rubbing eyes, arched back, fussiness that escalates, yawning, glazed look, sudden second wind
  • Prevention: stick to age-appropriate wake windows religiously
  • If already overtired: use all 5 S's, keep room very dark, minimize stimulation
  • Move bedtime earlier — 6:30–7 PM is often ideal for ages 4–12 months
  • Bedtime should follow last nap by ~1.5–2 wake windows, not by the clock
  • Baby wakes multiple times wanting pacifier replaced — this is a learned sleep prop
  • Option 1: teach baby to find and replace it — place multiple pacifiers in crib at 7+ months
  • Option 2: remove pacifier entirely and sleep train without it
  • If removing: cold turkey is typically faster (3–5 nights) than gradual reduction
  • True teething: brief, increased drooling, chewing, visible gum swelling, peaks for 2–3 days
  • Habit-based: consistent pattern every night at same intervals, persists for weeks
  • Do not assume all waking is teething — most night waking in healthy infants is behavioral
  • Comfort during genuine teething: brief check-in, infant Tylenol/ibuprofen if severe (per pediatrician)
  • Resume normal sleep approach as soon as teething pain resolves
  • Provide extra comfort during illness — do not sleep train a sick baby
  • Resume normal sleep approach within 1–2 days of recovery
  • Some babies bounce back immediately; others need 3–5 nights of gentle re-training
  • Use the gentlest effective method — often a mini-version of original sleep training

Safe Sleep ABC Critical

A · B · C
Alone Back Crib
  • Alone: no bed-sharing, no soft objects
  • Back: always on back to sleep
  • Crib: firm flat surface, no inclined sleepers, no positioners

Tired vs. Overtired Know This

Just Tired — Put Down Now
  • Yawning
  • Slowing down
  • Less vocal
  • Staring
  • Rubbing eyes gently
Overtired — Soothe First
  • Arching back
  • Inconsolable crying
  • Hyper-active second wind
  • Fist clenching
  • Eye rubbing + fussiness

Bedtime Routine Template (15–30 min)

Consistent sequence matters more than exact activities.
  1. Bath (optional — every other night is fine)
  2. Infant massage with lotion — 5–10 minutes
  3. Pajamas and sleep sack
  4. Feed (in dim light — avoid feeding to sleep after 4 months)
  5. Short book or lullaby in dark room
  6. White noise on, say consistent phrase (“goodnight, I love you, sleep well”)
  7. Place in crib drowsy but awake

Emergency Soothing Protocol (Crying Baby)

  1. Check for immediate needs — hunger, dirty diaper, pain (gas, reflux)
  2. Swaddle firmly if under 4 months
  3. Activate all 5 S's simultaneously — do not go one at a time
  4. Take baby outside or to a different room — change of environment can reset crying
  5. If nothing works, put baby safely in crib and take a 5-minute break — it is okay, and you need to stay regulated
⭐ A Note on Parental Wellbeing

Sleep deprivation impairs parental judgment, emotional regulation, and physical health at levels comparable to intoxication (Walker, Why We Sleep, 2017). Your sleep matters. A consistent sleep-trained baby is a gift to the whole family. Seeking help — sleep consultant, family support — is a sign of good parenting, not failure.
